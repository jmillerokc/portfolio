Rebuilding a Nurture Program Around Relevance

From generic sequences to dynamic content by vertical and role

A single high-performing email — a hospital case study sent to the healthcare segment — exposed what the rest of the nurture program was missing. The fix was not more volume or more channels, it was dynamic content built around the recipient's vertical and job title.

Segmenting nurture content by vertical and seniority drove material lift across the program:

01 / Discovery

One outlier email pointed at the real problem.

While analyzing email performance for a nurture campaign, one email in the sequence was performing well above benchmark in a single segment. Pulling on that thread reframed the entire program.

Signal
Identification

An email featuring a hospital group case study was being sent to the full list. Its overall numbers were unremarkable — but the healthcare segment alone was outperforming the rest of the list by 78% on open rate and click-through.

The content was not better. The relevance was. Healthcare readers saw a healthcare customer story and engaged. Everyone else saw a story about an industry that was not theirs.

The Outlier
+78%
Healthcare vs. rest of list

The hospital case study email

Same send, same cadence, same creative. The only difference was that the content matched the reader's industry. That single observation became the design principle for the rebuild — every nurture touch should look like it was written for that vertical and that role.

The Question
What does the rest of the nurture program look like if every email feels as relevant to its reader as the hospital case study felt to healthcare?
02 / Diagnosis

Sequence engagement was suppressing pipeline impact.

Initial assumptions blamed list quality or creative fatigue. Segmentation pointed somewhere else.

Channel
Diagnosis

One-off campaigns met or exceeded benchmark. Automated sequence emails consistently underperformed on both open rate and CTR. The issue was not channel-wide, it was isolated to nurture sequences.

Sequence Emails

Generic and non-contextual

Same copy went to every recipient regardless of industry or role. Engagement decayed as readers moved through the sequence and recognized less of themselves in each touch.

One-Off Sends

Hitting benchmark

Campaign-style sends, often built around a specific moment or asset, performed within range. The channel was not broken. The sequencing pattern was.

  • Sequences relied on generic, non-contextual messaging.
  • No differentiation by industry or role.
  • Engagement declined as messaging became less relevant.
03 / Intervention

Restructured sequences with contextual messaging.

Each touchpoint was aligned to industry-specific challenges and role-specific decision drivers using dynamic content.
