Case Study

Same demand. Different math.

Audit of a multi-brand pipeline that quietly stopped working

Two sister brands ran on the same shared CRM and the same shared sales motion. The leadership team was right that pipeline quality had dropped, but the obvious answer (we need more leads) was the wrong fix. Volume held. The pipeline thinned because the channel mix shifted, the auction math changed, and the CRM stopped recording the difference between a ghost and a no.

Eight thousand leads, thirty-nine months, two ad accounts. The signal was downstream of where the team was looking.

01 / Discovery

The complaint was real. The framing was wrong.

The client felt lead quality had dropped. The instinct was to push for more spend at the top of the funnel. Three quarters of inquiry data said something different.

Where the
signal lives

The pipeline runs through two stages. A first-touch rep handles every new inquiry. Anything that passes a fit and qualification threshold is handed to a senior closer who runs the deeper sales motion. Of the leads that ever advance past first contact, ninety-eight percent end up owned by that senior closer. So the percentage of leads that get to him is not a vanity metric. It is the operational definition of a sales-qualified opportunity for this business.

Plotted by quarter, that handoff rate sat in a tight band between twenty and twenty-three percent for two consecutive years. Then it broke.

What made this hard to see in the moment: total inquiry volume in the same window was almost identical to the prior year. 500-550 leads per quarter, then and now. The team was looking at the lead count and concluding the funnel was healthy. The ratio inside the funnel was the metric that had moved.

The Pattern
Same lead volume. Two-thirds the qualified handoffs. The funnel narrowed where nobody was watching.
02 / Diagnosis

Three things were happening at once.

The mix of channels feeding the pipeline shifted. A high-converting partner channel went dormant. And the CRM lost the ability to tell a non-responsive lead from a disqualified one.

What broke
and where

Splitting the inquiry data by source category produced a clean view. Per-channel conversion to senior pipeline stayed in the same neighborhood within each channel. What changed was how much each channel contributed to total volume. The cheapest, lowest-converting channel grew. The two best-converting channels shrank. The handoff rate moved with the mix.

The partner channel was the most striking finding. A small number of referral relationships had historically produced a handful of inquiries per quarter at a near-perfect handoff rate. Inside the analysis window, partner volume fell roughly fifty percent and one specific high-converting relationship effectively went dark. Recovering that channel alone, at its prior level, would close most of the gap to baseline without a single change to ad spend.

The CRM was hiding the second half of the story.

When a lead dies, the team marks it dead. The CRM offers a Lead Reason field for why. In the most recent twelve months, half of dead leads had been tagged with a single catch-all reason. Twenty-four percent had no reason at all. A second field that should record the stage at which the lead died had not been populated on a single record. The functional result is that there is no way, today, to distinguish a lead that was contacted, qualified, and declined from a lead that simply never responded. The two paths look identical in reporting.

That distinction matters because the fixes are completely different. Non-responders are a top-of-funnel pacing problem. Disqualified leads are a targeting or sales-process problem. Without the field discipline to tell them apart, the team cannot diagnose its own funnel.

Today

Single bucket for everything that died.

Half of dead leads carry the same vague reason. A quarter carry no reason at all. The "stage at killed" field is empty on every record.

Required

Structured outcome plus stage.

Required picklist for outcome (No Response, Disqualified Financial, Disqualified Geographic, Withdrew). Required field for the stage the lead was in at death. Free-text comments stay; they are the audit trail, not the data.

And the paid search account was inflating quietly.

Same total spend in 2025 as 2024. Roughly half the clicks. That single sentence is what made the third part of the diagnosis necessary, and it is where most of the recoverable money sits.

The Real Question
Same money, half the clicks. Where did the difference go, and why did Quality Score show no decline at all?
03 / Intervention

The auction changed underneath them.

Quality Score had not declined. Bids had. Splitting the account into Brand and Non-Brand showed where the entire CPC inflation actually lived.

Where the
money went

Both ad accounts showed the same surface pattern: spend roughly flat year over year, click volume cut in half, blended cost per click that looked like it had doubled. Disaggregating by campaign type produced a much sharper picture. Brand-search CPC stayed essentially flat. The cost per click on Non-Brand search had tripled on Account A and risen eighty-five percent on Account B. The "blended CPC doubled" framing was a composition effect: the cheap Non-Brand clicks that used to drag the average down had largely disappeared.

The Quality Score data ruled out the obvious explanation.

If CPC inflated, the textbook first guess is that ad relevance fell and Google began charging more for the same auction position. The Quality Score export refused that story. The Non-Brand campaigns had been running at the floor of the Quality Score scale for twenty-seven consecutive months. The campaigns weren't getting worse. They had always been mediocre. What changed was that the same mediocre auction position now cost three times as much to occupy.

That signature is consistent with one of two things, both of which we surfaced as the priority questions to take to the agency. A bid-strategy switch to a conversion-optimized algorithm with too little signal to learn from. Or a match-type and close-variant expansion that pulled the same low-quality keywords into wider, more competitive auctions. The two are not mutually exclusive.

Geographic targeting drifted in opposite directions on the two accounts.

One account got tighter. It was running in roughly two hundred ten regions in the baseline year and dropped to one hundred sixty-nine in the current cohort. Some of the removed regions were defensible cuts. Others were major metros where the brand has known sales territory available. Either way, a meaningful chunk of the remaining budget was sitting in regions that had produced zero conversions in the entire current year. Thirty-five percent of the account's spend in the trailing four quarters went to regions with zero qualified leads attributed.

The other account expanded sharply. A campaign that had run in seventy-two carefully selected regions for three consecutive quarters of the baseline year suddenly opened up to one hundred seventy regions in Q4 of that year and never tightened back. The new regions included rural and low-population markets that fall outside the brand's actual development territory. The expansion was almost certainly an attempt to recover volume during a budget shortfall in late 2024, and the configuration outlived the conditions that produced it.

The Lever
Quality Score didn't change. Bidding changed. The same auction position now costs three times as much, and the same regions produce nothing.

What this resolves to.

The intervention list separates work that pays back inside a quarter from work that resets the underlying system. The first list is short and concrete.

  • Cut spend in regions with zero conversions in the trailing twelve months. The cleanest single line item, with roughly a fifth of total paid budget on the table.
  • Add a comprehensive negative-keyword library on Non-Brand search. The top intent-disqualifying queries are visible in the search-term data and can be applied as exact-match negatives the same day.
  • Restore the wider account's Non-Brand campaign to its original curated region list, rather than the expanded version that was never tightened back.
  • Reactivate the dormant partner channel. The original relationship still exists; the volume does not.

The systemic work is harder and slower. Restructure the dead-lead taxonomy in the CRM so non-responders are distinguishable from disqualified leads. Pull the bid-strategy change history from the agency to confirm what shifted in early 2025. Rebuild the Non-Brand search campaigns with tighter match types and ad copy that actually addresses the buyer's question, since the current keyword and ad set has been operating at the bottom of the Quality Score scale for over two years.

The argument that came out of this work was not "you have a Google Ads problem." It was that demand was largely intact, the funnel had been quietly broken in three different places, and the team had been looking at the wrong number for nine months. The first three quick wins are visible in the data. The fourth, the CRM rework, is what makes the next audit possible.

The volume was fine. The bidding wasn't.
